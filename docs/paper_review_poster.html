Research Benchmark

What the Paper Review Tests

The Paper Review page compares dashboard evidence with outside housing-market ideas. The goal is not to copy a paper, but to show that the dashboard results can be judged against established theory and real-world conditions.

1. Theory Use the DiPasquale-Wheaton style framework: prices connect to demand, asset markets, construction, and supply.
2. Rates Check whether interest-rate or mortgage-rate pressure appears in correlations, ML, or forecast behavior.
3. Current Market Compare recent evidence with weak sales, changing inventory, high rates, and still-elevated prices.
4. Conclusion Write a balanced conclusion: pressure exists, but prices do not move because of one variable only.

The best project conclusion is balanced: the market can be pressured by affordability while still showing resilient prices when supply and timing matter.

Outside Theory

Four-Quadrant Housing Logic

  • Housing prices are connected to space demand, asset prices, construction, and long-run supply.
  • A realistic dashboard should not explain price movement with one simple chart.
  • Evidence is stronger when EDA, ML, OLAP, and historical periods point in the same direction.
Rate Pressure

Mortgage-Rate Research

  • Higher rates can reduce affordability and housing activity.
  • Prices may react slower than sales because inventory, timing, and supply shortages matter.
  • The dashboard checks this through rate features, model metrics, and forecast scenarios.
Current Market

Real-Life Comparison

  • Recent U.S. market context can show weak sales and high mortgage rates.
  • Prices can remain elevated when supply is constrained.
  • This supports a mixed-market interpretation instead of a simple crash-or-boom story.

Limitations

What the Poster Should Admit

  • Correlation is not causation.
  • National data can hide regional differences.
  • ML scores depend on selected features and the train/test split.
  • Forecasts are educational and should not be treated as financial advice.
Final Paper-Style Statement

Conclusion

This project supports a realistic housing-market interpretation: prices respond to affordability, interest rates, income, labor conditions, supply limits, and market timing together. Classic theory helps explain the expected relationships, but the dashboard adds value by testing those ideas with data quality checks, model evaluation, OLAP segmentation, scenario simulation, and production-readiness evidence.

The strongest final claim is not that one variable controls the housing market. The strongest claim is that housing prices require multiple evidence sources, and this dashboard gives those sources in one reproducible workflow.
